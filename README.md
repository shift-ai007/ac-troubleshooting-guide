# AC Troubleshooting Guide: Diagnosing Common Problems Before You Call a Technician

A practical, step-by-step guide for South Florida homeowners to diagnose common air conditioning problems. Knowing what's wrong before calling for service saves time, money, and keeps your home comfortable during the brutal summer months.

## Why Troubleshooting Matters in South Florida

South Florida's climate pushes AC systems harder than almost anywhere else in the country. With outdoor temps regularly hitting 95°F and humidity above 70%, your air conditioner runs 10–14 hours a day during peak summer. That constant load means components wear faster, refrigerant levels matter more, and small problems become big ones quickly.

The difference between a $150 service call and a $3,000 compressor replacement is often just catching the early warning signs.

## The Big Five: Most Common AC Problems

### 1. AC Running but Not Cooling

**Symptoms**: Air blows from vents but it's warm or barely cool. Thermostat shows the set temperature is much lower than room temperature.

**Likely causes**:
- **Dirty air filter** — This is the #1 cause. A clogged filter restricts airflow, which causes the evaporator coil to freeze. Check your filter first. In South Florida's dusty, pollen-heavy air, filters need replacing every 30–60 days, not the 90 days the packaging suggests.
- **Low refrigerant** — If the system was recently serviced and is low again, you likely have a leak. Refrigerant doesn't "burn off" — it circulates in a closed loop. Low refrigerant means a leak somewhere.
- **Dirty condenser coil** — The outdoor unit's coil collects dirt, leaves, and debris. If it can't release heat efficiently, cooling drops. Hose it down gently (not with a pressure washer).
- **Failed compressor capacitor** — The capacitor helps the compressor start. When it weakens, the compressor struggles and may short-cycle. You'll often hear a clicking or humming sound from the outdoor unit.

**What to do**: Replace the filter, clear debris from the outdoor unit, and check if the air coming from supply vents feels even slightly cool. If none of that helps, it's time for a professional diagnosis. ### 2. AC Short-Cycling (Turns On and Off Rapidly)

**Symptoms**: The system kicks on, runs for 2–5 minutes, shuts off, then restarts. Constant clicking from the thermostat.

**Likely causes**:
- **Oversized unit** — A system that's too large for your space cools too quickly, hitting the thermostat setpoint before properly dehumidifying. Common in South Florida where humidity matters as much as temperature.
- **Thermostat placement** — If the thermostat is near a window, vent, or in direct sunlight, it reads false temperatures.
- **Refrigerant leak** — Low refrigerant causes the low-pressure switch to trip, shutting down the compressor to prevent damage.
- **Dirty evaporator coil** — Restricts heat absorption, causing the system to overheat and shut down on the high-pressure safety.

**What to do**: Check the thermostat location and battery. Ensure vents aren't blocked by furniture. If the problem persists, a tech needs to check refrigerant pressure and coil condition.

### 3. Water Leaking from Indoor Unit

**Symptoms**: Water pooling around the air handler or dripping from the ceiling near the unit.

**Likely causes**:
- **Clogged condensate drain** — Florida's humidity means your AC produces 5–20 gallons of condensate per day. Algae, mold, and debris clog the drain line. This is the most common service call in South Florida during summer.
- **Cracked or overflowing drain pan** — Older units (10+ years) develop rust holes in the secondary drain pan.
- **Frozen evaporator coil thawing** — If the coil froze (from a dirty filter or low refrigerant) and now it's melting, you'll see water everywhere.

**What to do**: Find the condensate drain line (usually a PVC pipe near the outdoor unit). Try flushing it with vinegar or a wet/dry vacuum. If that doesn't work, the line may need professional clearing. Ignoring this causes water damage and mold — both serious problems in Florida's humidity.

### 4. Strange Noises from the System

**Symptoms**: Grinding, squealing, banging, or buzzing that wasn't there before.

| Sound | Likely Source |
|-------|--------------|
| Grinding | Blower motor bearings failing |
| Squealing | Belt slipping (older units) or compressor under stress |
| Banging/clanking | Loose component inside the blower or compressor |
| Buzzing | Electrical issue — contactor, capacitor, or loose wiring |
| Hissing | Refrigerant leak |
| Clicking (repeated) | Failed relay or control board |

**What to do**: Turn the system off immediately if you hear grinding or banging — continued operation can cause catastrophic damage. Buzzing and hissing warrant a same-day service call. For [commercial HVAC systems](https://ac-repair.today/services/commercial-hvac/), strange noises can indicate problems that affect entire buildings, so don't delay.

### 5. Uneven Cooling Between Rooms

**Symptoms**: Some rooms are comfortable, others are warm. Upstairs is always hotter.

**Likely causes**:
- **Duct leaks** — In South Florida attics that reach 140°F+, even small duct leaks waste enormous cooling capacity. Studies show 20–30% of conditioned air is lost through duct leaks in typical Florida homes.
- **Improper duct design** — Runs that are too long, too many sharp turns, or undersized ducts to certain rooms.
- **Closed or blocked vents** — Check all supply and return vents.
- **Single-zone system** — One thermostat controlling a multi-story home will always have temperature differences. Zoning systems or mini-splits solve this.

**What to do**: Walk through every room and check that vents are open and unobstructed. Feel the air at each vent — weak airflow suggests a duct problem. Duct sealing and insulation in the attic is one of the highest-ROI improvements for South Florida homes.

## The 5-Minute Pre-Call Checklist

Before you pick up the phone, run through this:

- [ ] **Thermostat**: Set to COOL, fan on AUTO, temperature set 3°+ below room temp
- [ ] **Air filter**: Pull it out and look. If you can't see light through it, replace it
- [ ] **Breakers**: Check both the indoor air handler breaker AND the outdoor unit breaker
- [ ] **Outdoor unit**: Is it running? Is it clear of debris? Is the fan spinning?
- [ ] **Vents**: All supply and return vents open and unblocked?
- [ ] **Drain line**: Any water around the indoor unit?
- [ ] **Age**: How old is the system? Units over 15 years are in the replacement zone

If you've checked all of these and the problem persists, you need a technician. The good news: having this information ready when you call means faster diagnosis and often a lower bill.

## When DIY Becomes Dangerous

Some things you should never attempt yourself:

- **Refrigerant handling** — Requires EPA 608 certification. Refrigerant can cause frostbite and displaces oxygen in enclosed spaces.
- **Electrical work** — AC systems run on 240V circuits. Fatal shock risk is real.
- **Compressor replacement** — Heavy, pressurized, requires specialized tools and brazing equipment.
- **Duct modification** — Improper duct changes destroy system balance and efficiency.

For anything beyond filter changes, thermostat adjustments, and drain line maintenance, call a licensed HVAC contractor.

## Preventive Maintenance: The South Florida Schedule

| Task | Frequency | DIY or Pro? |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Replace air filter | Every 30–60 days | DIY |
| Clear debris from outdoor unit | Monthly | DIY |
| Flush condensate drain | Every 3 months | DIY |
| Full system tune-up | Twice yearly (spring + fall) | Pro |
| Duct inspection | Every 2 years | Pro |
| Refrigerant level check | Annually | Pro |
| Electrical connection check | Annually | Pro |

Following this schedule prevents most emergency calls and extends system life by 3–5 years. In South Florida's demanding climate, skipping maintenance is like skipping oil changes on a car you drive 30,000 miles a year.
